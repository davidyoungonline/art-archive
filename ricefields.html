Ricefields

David Young with Sarah Pirrie & Rosemary Joy · installation / performance · 1998–1999
Presented by Aphids

The question behind Ricefields was simple, and it changed everything that came after: what happens when the musical score leaves the page?

I had been composing chamber music for years — notation on paper, performers reading from stands. The music was experimental, dense, alive to me, but it lived inside a convention that kept audiences at a distance. With Ricefields, the score became the room. Working with visual artists Sarah Pirrie and Rosemary Joy, we built a sculptural installation inspired by the rural and urban landscape of Matsue, Japan. I added graphic scores and performance instructions directly onto the installation elements — printed on a t-shirt, painted on a pole, embedded in the objects that surrounded the performers and the audience alike.

Photograph by David Young.

Four musicians interpreted this sculptural score live: Deborah Kayser on voice, Natasha Anderson on recorders, Peter Humble on percussion, and Yasutaka Hemmi — a violinist from Matsue — on violin and viola. Each came from a different musical background, from classical contemporary to jazz and improvisation. Michael Hewes processed their sound in real time, amplifying and transforming the acoustic material through live electronics — something that felt genuinely new at the time. Lisa Trewin lit the space. The audience moved through the installation, touching sushi-shaped sponges embedded with microphones, becoming part of the sounding environment.

What I discovered was that we had opened a door. The music I cared about most — experimental, challenging, uncompromising — could be presented in a way that invited people in rather than holding them at arm’s length. Audiences could enter the work, move through it, and trust their own experience of what they heard and felt. This was the breakthrough. It resonated through the next thirty years of Aphids projects, and it began here, at La Mama, in October 1998.

· · ·

Ricefields then travelled. First to Le Centre Chorégraphique in Belfort, France, in May 1999. Then, in October, to Japan — to Yumeshuraku in Shimane and Gallery 21 in Ginza, Tokyo — before returning to Australia for seasons at MetroArts in Brisbane and the Performance Space in Sydney. Each venue was different: a gallery, a theatre, a dance studio, a hall. The work evolved as it moved, absorbing the particular qualities of each space, adding new visual elements, adjusting the lighting to fit.

The Japan leg mattered most. Yasutaka Hemmi had come to Melbourne to perform with us; now we travelled to his country and performed in his landscape — the landscape that had inspired the work in the first place. This model of artist-to-artist exchange, built on personal connection rather than institutional arrangement, became one of the defining patterns of Aphids. I repeated it with Juliana Hodkinson in Copenhagen for Maps, and with Fritz Hauser and Boa in Basel for Schallmachine. It was a way of working that was simultaneously a business model, an artistic methodology, and a form of cultural exchange. It only worked because it was personal. Aphids could do it because Aphids wasn’t an institution — it was a set of deep, specific relationships between artists.

Publication

Sonorities of Site

Edited by Cynthia Troup · Aphids, St Kilda, 2012

A5 sketchbook format. Ricefields is one of five Aphids projects discussed in depth, with images, score samples, sketches and handwritten notes. Contributors include Rosemary Joy, Deborah Kayser, David Young, Anna Tweeddale, Juliana Hodkinson and Bronwyn Stocks. Designed by Paul Ducco and Jacob Thompson.
